About Microgrids

What is a Microgrid?

A microgrid is a localized group of electricity sources and loads that normally operates connected to and synchronous with the traditional centralized electrical grid (macrogrid). However, it has a crucial capability: it can disconnect from the macrogrid and operate autonomously in "island mode." This ensures that critical facilities can maintain power even when the main grid experiences outages.

Why are Microgrids Important?

Microgrids offer significant advantages, particularly in enhancing energy resilience and reliability. By being able to operate independently, they protect critical infrastructure and communities from widespread power disruptions caused by severe weather, equipment failures, or other grid disturbances.

Furthermore, microgrids facilitate the integration of distributed energy resources (DERs), such as solar panels, wind turbines, and battery storage systems. This promotes the use of renewable energy, reduces reliance on fossil fuels, and can potentially lower energy costs.

How do they Work?

A microgrid typically includes local power generation sources (like generators, solar PV), energy storage (batteries), and loads (buildings, equipment). A sophisticated control system manages these components, deciding when to connect or disconnect from the main grid, how to balance generation and load, and how to optimize energy use for resilience, cost savings, or environmental benefits.
